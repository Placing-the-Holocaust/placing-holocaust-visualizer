QUESTION: Good morning, Irene.

ANSWER: Good morning.

QUESTION: Tell me what your name is and spell your original last name and where and when you were born.

ANSWER: I am, I am born in Poland and my maiden name was hard to spell -- Salomonawicz -- and now I am Irene Fleming.

QUESTION: And what year were you born? When?

ANSWER: '21.

QUESTION: And the date?

ANSWER: February 5, 1921.

QUESTION: And you were born in Lodz ?

ANSWER: Yes.

QUESTION: Can you tell something about your family? When -- ANSWER: It was a traditional family -- [coughs] -- excuse me. We had a very good life. My father owned a factory . It was in the family for gen -- a few generations, and my grandmother and then my grandmother took in her two sons -- sons-in-laws -- into the business. We went to private schools . We had governesses at home . We had a good life.

QUESTION: And what kind of business was this?

ANSWER: Manufacturing textiles. You came into our factory naked, you got out dressed up from toe to head -- from head to toe, yeah.

QUESTION: So, it was, 1t was clothes?

ANSWER: It was underwear, yeah, and fabrics and sweaters and bathing suits. You were dressed when you left the place .

QUESTION: Did you go there very often?

ANSWER: Quite often I sneaked in, you know, and picked up what I liked and then brought it home and gave it to my friends.

QUESTION: And you had a brother?

ANSWER: I had a brother, yes.

QUESTION: How much younger was he than you?

ANSWER: Four years, four years younger than I was, and his dream -- my father wanted him to eventually take over the factory -- but since he was a little boy he was signing his name “Dr. Salomonawicz.” He wanted to be a physician and he became one after the war. He studied in Germany and became a physician.

QUESTION: You said you -- this was a traditional family. Were you very religious?

ANSWER: No. Just traditional. Very Jewish-conscious. My father was a Zionist and he was in very good company with a lot of very nice people.

QUESTION: And what sort of friends did you have? Did you have a lot of friends?

ANSWER: Oh, yes. We had a family, and, you know, when I was growing up life was different. People were caring and people cared about each other. If somebody got sick, the whole neighborhood would come to see if they can help. It was different. Now, everything is gone.

QUESTION: So you enjoyed your childhood?

ANSWER: Oh, yes, I did have a wonderful, I had a wonderful childhood.

QUESTION: Did you like school ?

ANSWER: Yes and no. If I liked the teacher, I liked the school . I, I would be a good pupil in his or her class. If I didn't like the teacher because I didn't like math, I was lousy at it, I just didn't do it. I was very bad at it. I had to have always tutor, a tutor to tutor me math so I can go from one grade to another.

QUESTION: What classes did you like?

ANSWER: Oh, I liked, I liked humanistic. I liked history, nature, and languages. This I liked. I was good at it.

QUESTION: Can you describe for us what a private school would have been like in, in Poland . Was, was this Jewish and non-Jewish?

ANSWER: No, this was a Jewish school and the name of the school was The Friends -- Friends of the of the Jerusalem University . The Friends organization still exists. They were the first ones to build on Mount Scopus , and this was the school . What was -- we had to wear uniforms so you didn't have jealously because there were quite a lot of kids that were from poor houses and they came to school for free and we didn't, they didn't want distinctions. And we had wonderful teachers on the faculty. The faculty, most of them had Ph.Ds. We had wonder -- the teachers were different. They were so committed to their pupils. They, even if you were a dumbbell they just want to, to get it into your brain, to, to get something out of you. They tried to get something out of you. They cared. Today, you know, it's just another salary.

QUESTION: Were you part of any youth groups growing up?

ANSWER: In school we had all kinds of youth groups that we belonged. As I said, it was a Jewish Hebrew school , a Hebrew School . We had wonderful teachers. Probably you know, you heard about Itzhak Katzenelson' and Max Braude was the director of our schools . There was one girls' school and two boys' schools . And we had wonderful teachers and we had - it was like a family. The whole school everybody knew each other.

QUESTION: Can you describe your, your mother?

ANSWER: My mother was a career woman. Very bright, very intelligent. She was fluent in five languages. Very pretty woman and she was running -- my father was traveling a lot and my mother was running the factory . So, she was, she was always away, busy. Friday night we would be together.

QUESTION: Is that unusual, to have a wife doing that sort of thing?

ANSWER: No, it wasn't, really. Where I lived there were a lot of women that helped their husbands in business or having their own business. I don't know, but we didn't have such gender differences because a woman was a woman. She had her brain and she used it as much as she could use it. Nobody wondered -- we had so many women doctors and dentists and lawyers, so it was nothing special. Nobody even thought about it. ANSWER: We, we listened to radio, you know. We had radio and then we had the news and the movies. We used to go a lot to the movies. You knew what's going on. My dad used to come home and say, "My God what an awful place . God should really have mercy on us because this is not going to be fun.” I saw these people how they struggle there. It can happen to anybody.

QUESTION: Were you worried?

ANSWER: Not really, you know, when you're young you look through different, you look like through pink glasses at everything. Nothing is really bad enough when you are young. You can withstand everything.

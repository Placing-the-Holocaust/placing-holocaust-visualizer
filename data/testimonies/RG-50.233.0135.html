QUESTION: Today is April 16, 1992. I’m Anthony Di Iorio. I’m at the home of Mr. Walter Taranowicz of ANSWER: Good morning. It’s nice to meet you.

QUESTION: My pleasure as well. Now where were you born?

ANSWER: I was born in New York City .

QUESTION: Well and when were you born?

ANSWER: July 22, 1918.

QUESTION: Were your parents American citizens?

ANSWER: My father was American citizen, my mother not.

QUESTION: Where was your mother born?

ANSWER: My mother was born in Poland .

QUESTION: And your father?

ANSWER: My father was born in Poland , too.

QUESTION: Where in Poland ?

ANSWER: My mother was born in southern states in Poland they call Galizia and my father was born in Apolashia in the eastern side of Poland .

QUESTION: How many brothers and sisters did you have?

ANSWER: I have one sister Stella.

QUESTION: Was she older than you or younger?

ANSWER: No, she’s younger than me.

QUESTION: Younger, so you were the oldest of two children?

ANSWER: Yes.

QUESTION: What did your father do for a living?

ANSWER: My father was a foreman in a factory over here in New York , I was watching.

QUESTION: And your mother?

ANSWER: And my mother was doing something in the factories , too. But when I went to Poland and saw my mother and her brother, he’s my uncle, was taking care of the big ranch what we had over there.

QUESTION: So your father’s family had a ranch in eastern Poland and your uncle was taking care of it.

ANSWER: No, uncle was taking care.

QUESTION: Your father was in New York ?

ANSWER: Yeah, and my father he came back to the United States , yeah, and he remained here and was planning later on come back to Poland , yeah but the war striked and he never came back.

QUESTION: You and your mother and your sister went back to Poland ?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: When did you go back?

ANSWER: It was the depression time over here. It was kind of not good so my father decide to take us to Poland and he set us off there that arranged and then he came back to make some more money.

QUESTION: Was your family 100% Polish and that everyone knew how to speak Polish?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: Did you also know how to speak English?

ANSWER: I was speaking English very good, yeah. Polish was poor. The Polish language when I went to public school , not great my Polish language but little by little I learned.

QUESTION: So you and your sister began to attend Polish schools in the 1930’s.

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: Had you attended any schools in the United States ?

ANSWER: Yeah, I was here in a school , in a grammar school .

QUESTION: How many years of school did you have in New York ?

ANSWER: About four or five.

QUESTION: And how many years of schooling did you get in Poland ?

ANSWER: In Poland I had three years.

QUESTION: Three years and then you finished school ?

ANSWER: No, wait, wait, I was in grammar school over there a couple of years and then I was in high school . That must be about five, six in the school there.

QUESTION: Did you complete school in Poland ?

ANSWER: The high school I completed.

QUESTION: And what was your specialty? What were you interested in?

ANSWER: General, all the subjects was in the high school . But then I was preparing myself to go to engineering and the war striked and no more school .

QUESTION: So you wanted to be an engineer?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: And where would you have gone to school , engineering college ?

ANSWER: Engineering College , in Poland .

QUESTION: In Poland ? In what city ? A? In Warsaw .

QUESTION: Now your relatives lived in eastern Poland .

ANSWER: Eastern Poland .

QUESTION: And you lived on a farm or on a ranch ?

ANSWER: It’s in a ranch , in a ranch .

QUESTION: What did they raise? What did they grow on this farm ?

ANSWER: Horses and cattle and honey and fish. Fish was a carps; it was a lot of them.

QUESTION: And was your mother the one who ran this ranch or your uncle?

ANSWER: Uncle. My mother and uncle they both was running that ranch . The uncle is a man he was more in it every place .

QUESTION: And you planned on becoming an engineer. Did you plan on returning to the United States ? A? Yeah, I thought I’d get European education and I come back to the United States and remain here.

QUESTION: Now did you pay any attention to politics while you were going to school in Poland ?

ANSWER: Not much, not much was involved with the politics.

QUESTION: Had you heard of the Nazis? Had you heard of Hitler?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: And what did you know of him?

ANSWER: Oh, about him and Poland at that time that he was armed up to his ears and he took Austria , he took Czechoslovakia and he’s after the Polish, that corridor to the sea .

QUESTION: Were you afraid that there might be a war?

ANSWER: Nobody expected that. Nobody believed it in school all over where I was. Nobody believed in that war was going to strike. Q? So your mother felt safe on the ranch ?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: Your father in America , did he get worried about a possible war?

ANSWER: Yeah, he said that he more or less was informed of here in the United States 1s possible that Hitler is going to strike the big war.

QUESTION: Against Poland ?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: But no one in Lublin was worried?

ANSWER: Nobody in Poland was thinking about it.

QUESTION: Were you at any point liable for military service?

ANSWER: No, I was too young.

QUESTION: Where were you when war broke out in 1939?

ANSWER: I was on a ranch at that time.

QUESTION: Your father’s family’s ranch ?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: Do you recall any military actions near your ranch ?

ANSWER: No, was no military actions at all.

QUESTION: You were far away from....?

ANSWER: Far away from the front line and suddenly one morning we see the Russian troops are coming.

QUESTION: Russian troops? Was this expected or was this a complete surprise?

ANSWER: Complete surprise.

QUESTION: And what was your reaction to these Russian troops?

ANSWER: I didn’t like it because as a student I head a lot about the Russian’s Communism and Communism the Polish people didn’t like it and I didn’t like it, too. And suddenly we see there are some troops come in and taking over all the eastern Poland .

QUESTION: What else did they teach you in school besides that Communism was a bad thing? Did they teach you anything else?

ANSWER: The history, geography, the Polish language, religion, sciences, many subjects. Many subjects in school .

QUESTION: Was your family Catholic?

ANSWER: Catholic, yeah.

QUESTION: Were all the people in your area Catholic?

ANSWER: No. There was I would say about 20 % Catholics and 80% was Russian Orthodox.

QUESTION: How were relations between Polish Catholics and the Russian Orthodox?

ANSWER: It was very friendly. In regions where I was very friendly the Russian Orthodox and the Polish Catholics was friendly.

QUESTION: How about your ranch ? Who worked on your ranch ?

ANSWER: Russians.

QUESTION: Russians. Ukrainian?

ANSWER: No, was no Ukrainians, Russians, They called themselves White Russians.

QUESTION: You had friends who were White Russian and Polish?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: What happened to your ranch after the Russians came 1n?

ANSWER: Well when the Russians came in first thing they grabbed all the horses, twelve horses they took it. Then they took the cattles. They was after everything.

QUESTION: So your family lost the farm ?

ANSWER: Yeah. Then before Hitler strikes into Russia in ’41, Russians came with the truck , loaded my mother and my sister and little bit belongings what they had and took them on the railroad station to the cattle wagons and deported my mother and sister and other Polish people to Siberia .

QUESTION: What was their crime?

ANSWER: Bourgeoia, rich. QW: They were bourgeois.

ANSWER: Bourgeois.

QUESTION: The fact that your sister was an American citizen. Did that make any difference?

ANSWER: No, it didn’t make no difference.

QUESTION: Did they have passports?

ANSWER: Sister, yes.

QUESTION: What happened to her passport?

ANSWER: Disappeared in Russians ??

QUESTION: So your mother and your sister were deported to Siberia ?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: And what about you?

ANSWER: I, in the beginning, run away to the German side intending to get in touch with American Consulate in Warsaw which was no consulate , no embassy in Poland only in Berlin but the Germans don’t let me go to Berlin so I remain with my friends in Miendvyrec Podlaski .

QUESTION: And how far away is that from the ranch ?

ANSWER: About 25 miles to the border .

QUESTION: Were you on the German side of the border or the....?

ANSWER: On the German side .

QUESTION: So when your mother and your sister were taken away you weren’t there?

ANSWER: No, I wasn’t there.

QUESTION: Do you remember when they were taken away?

ANSWER: Summertime, I know they told me. Summertime and in 1941.

QUESTION: 1941, before the Germans came?

ANSWER: Yeah, before the Germans came.

QUESTION: Now what happened to you?

ANSWER: I was already in the Nazi’s jail .

QUESTION: And when were you arrested by the Nazis?

ANSWER: January, January 13, 1940.

QUESTION: And what was your crime?

ANSWER: No crime, American spy, saboteur.

QUESTION: You were a spy...?

ANSWER: And a saboteur, yeah. Where I told me the good Americans all in America only the American swines are here to do some rotten jobs.

QUESTION: And what had you been doing?

ANSWER: I wasn’t doing nothing. I was a student. I was a small kid.

QUESTION: You were a kid. How old were you when they arrested you?

ANSWER: I was I think eighteen, nineteen.

QUESTION: Eighteen, nineteen years old and guilty of spying and sabotage.

ANSWER: Yeah, and sabotage.

QUESTION: And you hadn’t committed sabotage?

ANSWER: No, commit nothing against the Germans.

QUESTION: Who were you living with when they arrested you?

ANSWER: I was living with a Doctor Demoraski, his son -- his son was my friend from the school -- so I was living with Doctor Demoraski’s family in Miendvyrec .

QUESTION: And had they been engaged in any sabotage?

ANSWER: They had been arrested at the same time.

QUESTION: And had they done anything against the German government?

ANSWER: No.

QUESTION: What were your feeling toward the Germans when they occupied Poland ?

ANSWER: Well, I didn’t know the Germans that good till I got arrested and beated, beaten up, then I got to know how bad are the Germans.

QUESTION: So you were beaten up?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: Why were you beaten up?

ANSWER: To sign a confession that I am saboteur and spy.

QUESTION: Did you sign?

ANSWER: I did not sign, no documents.

QUESTION: What did they do to you when they beat you up?

ANSWER: Oh, they beat me up boxing, beating up with the straps, with the fists and then with some kind of baseball sticks on my feet, yeah.

QUESTION: On your feet?

ANSWER: On my feet, on my toes, yeah. I had black and blue feet, couldn’t walk for a couple weeks.

QUESTION: And what happened to your American passport?

ANSWER: My American passport, they took 1t away yeah and I never see it again my passport.

QUESTION: And how long did this go on, this series of beatings?

ANSWER: Oh the investigation? One week they were torturing me. Every day little bit, every day little bit, every day little bit for one week. Then they give up.

QUESTION: Did your mother and sister know that you had been arrested?

ANSWER: No, when they was in Siberia then they got the news that I am in Auschwitz .

QUESTION: But before that they didn’t know that you had been arrested?

ANSWER: They didn’t know, no.

QUESTION: Who was arrested first you or your mother and your sister?

ANSWER: I was arrested first.

QUESTION: You were arrested first and then shortly after that they were arrested.

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: And they were already in Siberia when you were sent to Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: When I was in Auschwitz , yeah.

QUESTION: And after these beatings were you transferred to another prison ?

ANSWER: Yeah, that was in Lukov that investigation and beating and tortures. Then from Lukov they transferred us to Lublin , Lublin jail called Nazanku .

QUESTION: And what happened in this jail ?

ANSWER: That jail was a terrible thing over there. Every night, every night they was calling from the cells some prisoners and shoot them 1n the yard .

QUESTION: And who were these prisoners?

ANSWER: The Polish prisoners.

QUESTION: All Polish? A; All Polish. Q; No Russian Orthodox? A; No. Q; And what were they accused of by the Germans?

ANSWER: Underground work.

QUESTION: Were you aware of any underground activities before you were arrested?

ANSWER: No, no.

QUESTION: And how long were you kept in this prison in Lublin ?

ANSWER: About three, four months in Lublin .

QUESTION: And then what happened?

ANSWER: Then one day, sunny day, I know it was nice and warm day they put is in the trucks with the big army escort then to the railroad station and the cattle wagons . We went straight to Auschwitz . First they told us we’re going to Germany for a work but then we end up in Auschwitz .

QUESTION: How many of you were shipped to Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: Oh, it was about 5,000 men, 5,000 men that transport to Auschwitz .

QUESTION: All men?

ANSWER: All men.

QUESTION: And were they all Poles?

ANSWER: All Polish.

QUESTION: No Ukrainians, no White Russians?

ANSWER: No, no.

QUESTION: And what happened when you arrived in Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: When we arrived to Auschwitz so was a big gate in Auschwitz , “Arbeit Macht Frei’ and orchestra was playing... QUESTION: There was an orchestra playing when you arrived?

ANSWER: Yeah, orchestra was playing when we was marching into the camp .

QUESTION: Who was playing in this orchestra?

ANSWER: The prisoners.

QUESTION: Prisoners. How big of an orchestra was it?

ANSWER: Oh surprise big orchestra. Then we all was standing up ten in a line , how to explain you that?

QUESTION: In rows of ten?

ANSWER: In rows of ten, yeah.

QUESTION: Of ten, not of five?

ANSWER: No, ten, and then the camp commandant came in and told us a big speech that we are no more Walter or Johnny or some other name we all are numbers and the number is very easy to be erased and if you gonna behave you may see the freedom. If you don’t behave your freedom 1s gonna go through the chimney (he pointed chimney to the crematorium ). Your freedom’s gonna go through that chimney .

QUESTION: What was your number?

ANSWER: 14, 526.

QUESTION: And when did you arrive in Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: In 1941.

QUESTION: Do you remember the month?

ANSWER: I think it was January, January 13.

QUESTION: It was cold?

ANSWER: It was cold, yeah.

QUESTION: And what else happened to you when you arrived -- give you uniform?

ANSWER: Yeah, they took us to the shower , take all your clothes out and they give us the camp uniform and they divide in certain blocks . I end up in a block number seventeen right by the kitchen , block seventeen.

QUESTION: Besides given a number, were you given any kind of insignia or badges to put on your uniform?

ANSWER: Yeah, they give three angle, red with a P on it.

QUESTION: Red triangle so you were a political prisoner?

ANSWER: Red as a political.

QUESTION: Even though you hadn’t done anything?

ANSWER: Even though I didn’t do nothing, no, didn’t do nothing.

QUESTION: Were there other American citizens, do you know of?

ANSWER: Yes, there was three of us. Was Chenckk, Walter Chenchek and Domashevski. Domashevski is someplace in New Jersey . The Chenchek was here in Westchester County and he had two bureaus on Long Island , one in Riverhead , one in Hempstead . And with the Chenchek I was a very close friend. But Chenchek died about three years ago.

QUESTION: And were they arrested in Lublin like you?

ANSWER: No, no, Chenchek was arrested in Warsaw and Domashaveski was arrested someplace in Radom . They’re different cities .

QUESTION: So you met them in Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: Yeah, met them in Auschwitz .

QUESTION: Did you ever try to complain or protest?

ANSWER: Oh, yes.

QUESTION: In Auschwitz or?

ANSWER: In Auschwitz so I got slapped in the face and kicked in the ass and shut up, don’t say nothing.

QUESTION: Were there any acts of resistance either during the transport or in Auschwitz itself?

ANSWER: No.

QUESTION: And what did you have to do when you were there?

ANSWER: In Auschwitz we was building the barracks . I called 1t Noibaum group, building the barracks . It’s hard work but then the camp commandant and the doctor took all the teenagers and sent us to the trade shops and I end up in a cabinet shop .

QUESTION: Do you know where you were building these new barracks ?

ANSWER: In Auschwitz . There was about half of the barracks and most of them was one story but then we build the second story on it and build the new ones and outside the camp a lot of building was coming up too.

QUESTION: What barracks did you sleep in?

ANSWER: I was sleeping in barrack number seventeen .

QUESTION: Were these made of wood or made of brick?

ANSWER: Made of brick, everything was made of brick.

QUESTION: And the ones you were building , were they made of brick or wood?

ANSWER: Brick.

QUESTION: So this is the old part of the camp ?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: And were you seeing a lot of construction in the distance? Is the camp getting bigger at this time?

ANSWER: Yeah, at the time even though was big group was going near the Auschwitz they called it Majdanek , Majdanek . That was new camp not far from the Auschwitz so the group was going over there and daytime was working and night was coming back. But meantime already I left the Auschwitz and I don’t know what happened but I learn later that was a very big new camp over there. QW: Had you heard of Birkenau ?

ANSWER: Birkenau , oh yes.

QUESTION: Was that the place that you’d seen or heard of when you were in Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: Birkenau or they call it Majdanek , yeah?

QUESTION: Majdanek as I recall, Majdanek was near Lublin . That was a different camp.

ANSWER: Oh yeah, I’m mixed up, mixed up. It’s over here in Oswiecim they call it, Auschwitz . They call it Birkenau , Brzezinka , sometime like that.

QUESTION: That’s the one. So you were selected to become a cabinetmaker? Had you volunteered?

ANSWER: As acabinetmaker, yes. He was asking every teenager what you want to be, what kind of trade. I said I wanted to be a cabinetmaker so okay, yeah.. QUESTION: Had you ever done that before?

ANSWER: No.

QUESTION: What made you pick this new trade?

ANSWER: Because it’s nice and warm over there and it was interesting for me. But the main thing I was looking for where it’s nice and comfortable, warm.

QUESTION: Who taught you to be a cabinetmaker?

ANSWER: Was from Warsaw Mr. Buchwald name, real old guy and good cabinetmaker and I was apprenticed attached to his bench . He was a cabinetmaker, I was apprentice and he was teaching me. Q; Hw was a prisoner?

ANSWER: A prisoner, yeah, he was a prisoner.

QUESTION: Polish?

ANSWER: Polish, named Buchwald.

QUESTION: And how long were these lessons? How long did this last?

ANSWER: I was with him close to two years.

QUESTION: Two years?

ANSWER: Yeah, close to two years with him.

QUESTION: In Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: In Auschwitz , yeah.

QUESTION: And then what happened?

ANSWER: Then I volunteer to go to another camp because too much was executions in Auschwitz , too much executions and diseases. So I volunteered to go to another camp and they picked me up and I end up in Mauthausen-Gusen .

QUESTION: Now when you were in Auschwitz were you working on cabinets ?

ANSWER: In Auschwitz I was a cabinetmaker.

QUESTION: So you actually built cabinets ?

ANSWER: Yeah, apprentice building the cabinets and most of the things for army -- soldiers beds , soldiers racks for rifles and tables and benches for army supplies.

QUESTION: So these were not for prisoners at Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: No.

QUESTION: These were for the Germans?

ANSWER: For Germans.

QUESTION: Now what did you see at the camp ? You said there were a lot of beatings and killings and so forth. Does anything in particular come to mind when you think of these years?

ANSWER: Yes, every day there was beating and killing and smashing the prisoners, executions almost every day, every day a pill, yeah and they called the numbers going to work so what happens they take them outside the camp and executions, shoot them. And I think I remember by the end I was there they brought twelve thousand Russian war prisoners to Auschwitz and as war prisoners, it was winter time, December, they was twenty four hours naked and a very cold night and snow even. Then they put them into barracks and gave them some kind of clothing but the food was very, very little so through the winter they almost all died out. There was only a few hundred left of those war prisoners.

QUESTION: Russians?

ANSWER: Russians, yeah.

QUESTION: Were they put to work?

ANSWER: No, no.

QUESTION: So they were just allowed to die?

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: What about the rest of the prisoners there, who were they?

ANSWER: Rest of the prisoners was Polish, Czechs, Germans, Hungarians, we had some French, few British, three of us American citizens was there and Spanish, Spanish was there.

QUESTION: You mentioned British. Were these British prisoners of war or British civilians?

ANSWER: No, British civilians. It’s like me.

QUESTION: How had they been caught?

ANSWER: They’d been caught in Germany , in French, and one was caught in Poland .

QUESTION: Were they treated any better or any worse than the others?

ANSWER: No.

QUESTION: Were there any Gypsies there when you were there?

ANSWER: Oh yeah, there was Gypsies.

QUESTION: And how were they treated?

ANSWER: Gypsies were not treated good, wasn’t treated good.

QUESTION: Was anybody treated good?

ANSWER: No, the worst treatment was the Gypsies and the priests. There was couple, German couples., Kickerman his name was. He was big Communist and he find out any prisoner is a priest so he finish him up in a couple of days. So the priest was scared to come up openly that he is a priest.

QUESTION: So your kapo was a German Communist?

ANSWER: Yeah. All the kapos was Germans and most of them was with the black triangle.. QUESTION: Were there any Jews?

ANSWER: Black and some was with the lilac color, lilac they was the religious, the Baptist.

QUESTION: Jehovah’s Witnesses.

ANSWER: Jehovah’s Witnesses, yeah.

QUESTION: How were they? Did you know any Jehovah’s Witnesses?

ANSWER: They was all right, they was all right. So far as I seen them they was not nasty. They was doing what they had to do but they was not nasty, they was all right.

QUESTION: Were there any Jews in this camp ?

ANSWER: Yes. There was one block with Jews. They call it Special Commando. The kapo was a Jew and the block , kapo, everything was Jewish over there. And they was going to work outside the camp but separate from us.

QUESTION: And you were in Auschwitz for how long?

ANSWER: For about two, two and a half years.

QUESTION: So you were first arrested we said in January?

ANSWER: January 13 in 1940 in Miendvyrec Polaskti .

QUESTION: And you were transferred in Mauthausen-Gusen , do you remember when you were transferred?

ANSWER: 42, yeah, ’42, month I don’t remember but it was in ’42.

QUESTION: And how many from your barrack or your camp were transferred with you?

ANSWER: It was about 4,000 men transferred from Auschwitz to Mauthausen-Gusen .

QUESTION: How did you go there?

ANSWER: “By train and up in the Mauthausen railroad station we walk a couple of miles through the Mauthausen city to the Mauthausen camp which was there under quarantine for a week, about one week under quarantine. Then we marched on foot a few miles, about five miles to the Gusen camp .

QUESTION: What kind of train did you travel on?

ANSWER: Cattle, cattle wagons .

QUESTION: How many people in each wagon ?

ANSWER: Full, as many as they push it in. Was like sardines in a can. If you want to lay down or sit down there was no room -- all you have to stand up.

QUESTION: Did you smell better or worse than sardines?

ANSWER: Oh, we smelled, smelled.

QUESTION: Did you have any food?

ANSWER: They gave us one loaf of bread and some kind of cheese.

QUESTION: Did you have any kind of toilet facilities?

ANSWER: No, we stood in the corner of the wagon .

QUESTION: No barrel, nothing?

ANSWER: No.

QUESTION: And this must have lasted a few days.

ANSWER: Yeah.

QUESTION: What were you lead to expect? What did they tell you you would find in Mauthausen-Grusen ?

ANSWER: Well they told us we’re going to different camp , we’re going to different camp .

QUESTION: How did the new camp compare with the old camp ?

ANSWER: The new camp felt much better and easier than Auschwitz .

QUESTION: In what ways?

ANSWER: There was no beating, no killing, no executions, the food was little bit better but the work, the hard work, the hard work and the granite stones, that was killing us.

QUESTION: So you weren’t a cabinetmaker anymore?

ANSWER: No, I was working a few months with the granite stones carrying the little and bigger stones, loading up the wagons and finally I was very weak and was eating my lunch, the soup, and finally somebody comes and pat me on the shoulder and said, how are you son? I look at him and some gentleman start talking with me. Who are you? From where do you come? From Auschwitz . From where? From New York . You are American? Yes. What was you doing in Auschwitz ? Cabinetmaker. Are you know how to be cabinetmaker? I learn a little bit. I can do easy things. All right, you want to be cabinetmaker? Sure, I don’t want to be here in the granite stones. SoI gonna try to get you to the cabinet shop . And his name was Luzion Buchwald. He was one of the first prisoners and he was an architect in the camp and he talked with the Arbeitgenes, that’s the Secretary of the Labor and in a couple of days I was in a cabinet shop . And thanks to cabinet shop and Luzion Buchwald I’m alive today.

QUESTION: Now this Luzion Buchwald was a prisoner, do you know from which country ?

ANSWER: He was Polish, from Warsaw . He was an architect from Poland and he was now in the camp as an Architect Bureau and doing some kind of architectural work for the Nazis.

QUESTION: I remember you mentioned someone, the person that you were apprenticed with in Auschwitz , this person wasn’t...?

ANSWER: No, was a Karl Buchwald too.

QUESTION: They weren’t related?

ANSWER: Was not related, no.

QUESTION: A happy coincidence apparently. So how long were you working in the quarry before you received this promotion?

ANSWER: About three months.

QUESTION: If you hadn’t been transferred how long do you think you would have lasted?

ANSWER: If I wouldn’t be transferred to the cabinet shop I think another five, six months.

QUESTION: And what?

ANSWER: And then I would die. I was getting skinnier and skinnier and I was afraid I would get sick and if you get sick there’s no hope. You go to the hospital and instead of cure you they kill you over there.

QUESTION: How about the barracks where you were sleeping? Were they better or worse than in Auschwitz ?

ANSWER: The barracks I would say was comfortable. Was a wooden barracks , three stories beds and not crowded as was in Auschwitz .

QUESTION: Did you sleep alone in your bed ?

ANSWER: Alone, yeah.

QUESTION: So one person in each bunk ?

ANSWER: Yeah in each bunker which in Auschwitz was two in a bunker .

QUESTION: How long did you remain in Mauthausen ?

ANSWER: In Mauthausen , one week.

QUESTION: And then to Gusen ?

ANSWER: We marched to the Gusen .

QUESTION: And how long were you in Gusen ?

ANSWER: Another two years.

QUESTION: Again did you ever complain or try to get out?

ANSWER: No, never came to my mind try to escape because I seen if a few guys tried to escape they got caught and killed so I never tried, don’t think to escape. Thinking that it’s gonna end some day, the war is gonna end and everything is gonna be solved.

QUESTION: So you were hopeful that this would not last forever?

ANSWER: Yeah.
